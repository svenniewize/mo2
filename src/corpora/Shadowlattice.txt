---

## **5.1 What Constraint Fields Are**

Constraint fields are:

- not beliefs  
- not norms  
- not laws  
- not explicit rules  

They are **pre‑conceptual boundary conditions**.

They define:

- what counts as “possible”  
- what counts as “real”  
- what counts as “thinkable”  
- what counts as “allowed”  
- what counts as “me” or “not‑me”  

You don’t *hold* a constraint field.  
You **live in** it.

It’s the water you swim in, not the fish you notice.

---

## **5.2 The Three Classes of Constraint Fields**

There are three primary classes:

1. **Ontological Constraints** – what is allowed to be real  
2. **Normative Constraints** – what is allowed to be acceptable  
3. **Experiential Constraints** – what is allowed to be felt

Each of these operates *beneath* thought and language.

---

### **5.2.1 Ontological Constraints**  
**What is allowed to exist.**

These determine:

- what kinds of entities are “real”  
- what kinds of explanations are “valid”  
- what categories are “natural”  

Examples (shadow‑level, not explicit):

- “Inner states are less real than outer events.”  
- “Only what can be measured matters.”  
- “Other people’s perspectives are more valid than mine.”  
- “Certain experiences are ‘just imagination’ and therefore ignorable.”  

These are not philosophies you adopt; they’re **reality‑filters** you inherit.

---

### **5.2.2 Normative Constraints**  
**What is allowed to be acceptable.**

These govern:

- what behaviors are “okay”  
- what identities are “safe”  
- what expressions are “appropriate”  

Examples:

- “Anger is not allowed here.”  
- “Needing help is shameful.”  
- “Wanting is selfish.”  
- “Taking up space is dangerous.”
