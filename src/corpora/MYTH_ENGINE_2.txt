Mythic cognition refers to the set of cognitive processes through which human minds generate, organize, and interact with symbolic structures. 1.2 The Function of Myth
Myth serves several cognitive and psychological functions:

Compression: Myth condenses complex emotional and experiential material into symbolic form.
Orientation: Myth provides frameworks for interpreting internal and external events.
Transformation: Myth enables psychological change through symbolic descent and integration.
Communication: Myth transmits affective and existential truths across individuals and generations.
Stabilization: Myth organizes chaotic or overwhelming experiences into coherent structures.
These functions emerge spontaneously in human cognition, even in the absence of formal religious or cultural frameworks.

1.3 Myth vs. Symbol vs. Archetype
Although often used interchangeably, these terms refer to distinct layers of the symbolic system.

Symbol
A symbol is a discrete unit of meaning that represents or evokes something beyond itself. Symbols may be visual, auditory, conceptual, or affective.

Archetype
An archetype is a symbolic process, not a fixed entity. It is a dynamic pattern of behavior, emotion, and meaning that emerges from repeated symbolic compression. Archetypes are stable enough to be recognized but fluid enough to evolve.

Myth
Myth is the narrative and relational field in which symbols and archetypes interact. It is the ecosystem of meaning that arises when archetypes form relationships, tensions, and cycles.

The Myth‑Engine models how these layers emerge, stabilize, and interact.
